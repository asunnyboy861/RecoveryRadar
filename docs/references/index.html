Muscle Recovery & Training

Exponential Recovery Model

The recovery algorithm uses an exponential decay model based on the principle that muscle recovery follows a non-linear time course, with faster initial recovery that slows over time. This is consistent with research on muscle protein synthesis and recovery kinetics.

Schoenfeld, B. J. (2010). The mechanisms of muscle hypertrophy and their application to resistance training. Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research, 24(10), 2857-2872.

Damas, F., et al. (2016). A review of resistance training-induced changes in skeletal muscle protein synthesis and their contribution to hypertrophy. Sports Medicine, 46(6), 809-818.

EMG-Driven Muscle Activation Mapping

Muscle activation patterns during exercises are mapped using electromyography (EMG) research data to determine which muscles are primarily engaged during specific workout types.

Schoenfeld, B. J. (2011). The mechanisms of muscle hypertrophy and their application to resistance training. Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research, 24(10), 2857-2872.

Wernbom, M., Augustsson, J., & Thomee, R. (2007). The influence of frequency, intensity, volume and mode of strength training on whole muscle cross-sectional area in humans. Sports Medicine, 37(3), 225-264.

Contreras, B., et al. (2016). Muscle activation during low- and high-load resistance training in well-trained men. Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research, 30(3), 736-744.

Rest Day Recommendations

Training suggestions are based on research showing that adequate rest between training sessions for the same muscle group is essential for optimal recovery and performance. The 48-72 hour recovery window is widely supported in sports science literature.

Kraemer, W. J., & Ratamess, N. A. (2004). Fundamentals of resistance training: progression and exercise prescription. Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise, 36(4), 674-688.

American College of Sports Medicine. (2009). Position stand: progression models in resistance training for healthy adults. Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise, 41(3), 687-708.

Heart Rate Variability (HRV)

HRV as a Recovery Indicator

Heart rate variability (HRV) is used as an auxiliary metric to assess autonomic nervous system recovery. Lower HRV generally indicates higher stress and incomplete recovery, while higher HRV suggests better recovery status.

Buchheit, M. (2014). Monitoring training status with HR measures: do all roads lead to Rome? Frontiers in Physiology, 5, 73.

Plews, D. J., et al. (2013). Analysis of heart rate variability in elite distance runners. International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance, 8(1), 32-40.

Gronwald, T., et al. (2022). Heart rate variability in sports and exercise medicine. Deutsche Zeitschrift fur Sportmedizin, 73(1), 10-19.

Overtraining Prevention

Overtraining Syndrome

The app's fatigue indicators are designed to help users avoid overtraining by monitoring cumulative training load and recovery status across multiple muscle groups.

Meeusen, R., et al. (2013). Prevention, diagnosis, and treatment of the overtraining syndrome. European Journal of Sport Science, 13(1), 1-24.

Kellmann, M., et al. (2018). Recovery and performance in sport: consensus statement. International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance, 13(2), 240-245.
